US election 2020

On November 3rd, Americans will cast their ballots for the next US President, and will also vote for members of Congress in the Senate and House elections.

This dashboard provides an overview of the elections taking place this year, including projections for the Presidency, Senate and House, as well as showing results from 2016 and the current breakdown of Congress.

Projections are taken from the latest Cook Political Ratings. For more information, click the 'Methodology' button above.

Presidency

Donald Trump faces Joe Biden in the Presidential race, in which a candidate needs to obtain a majority of 270 of the 538 electoral votes that are divided between states.

Current polling has Biden ahead, but 2020 has brought new dynamics such as coronavirus and the increased importance of mail-in ballots. These challenges bring more uncertainty, and with Trump pulling off an unexpected win in 2016, pollsters are wary of underestimating his ability to make a comeback in 2020.

Senate

It's not all about the Presidential race. For a bill to be made into law, it must pass through both the Senate and the House. Therefore, having control of both parts of Congress in addition to the Presidency provides ultimate power in Washington - something the Democrats will be hoping to gain in 2020.

Made up of 100 Senators (two for each State), the Senate is currently held by the Republicans with a majority of 53-47. Only a third of Senate seats are up for election every two years. This year there are 35 seats up for election, with the Democrats needing to pick up three or four to gain a majority.

House

The House of Representatives has 435 voting members, divided between states by population, with each district represented by one member.

Currently held with a 232-197 majority by the Democrats, it looks as though they will retain control in 2020 when all seats go up for election on November 3rd.

Methodology

Projections are sourced from Cook Political Ratings, correct to the date displayed below each graphic. Electoral College Ratings are scraped from the PDF provided by Cook, using Tabula, while Senate and House projects are scraped using Python with BeautifulSoup. The scripts for Senate and House web scraping can be found in the GitHub repo for this project.

2016 Presidential results and electoral college vote counts for each state are sourced from the Federal Election Commission.

Information on the current breakdowns of the House and Senate are sourced from Congress.gov.
